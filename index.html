80/20 Rule

A high percentage of effects in any large system
are caused by a low percentage of variables.

Do not do this!

  • 80% of a product's usage involves 20% of its features.
  • 80% of a town's traffic is on 20% of its roads.
  • 80% of a company's revenue comes from 20% of its products.
  • 80% of innovation comes from 20% of the people.
  • 80% of progress comes from 20% of the effort.
  • 80% of errors are caused by 20% of the components.

80/20 Rule

  • Focus resources on core features
  • Improve efficiency of core features
  • Minimize effort on noncritical features
  • Emphasize core features
  • Make noncritical features harder to find

Aesthetic-Usability Effect

Aesthetic designs are easier to use
than less-aesthetic designs.

Aesthetic designs

  • Look easier to use
  • Are easily accepted
  • Make people more tolerant to problems

Less-aesthetic designs

  • Look harder to use
  • Are not easily accepted
  • Make people more intolerant of problems

But do not sacrifice usability for aesthetics!
Where is room 201?

Aesthetic-Usability Effect

  • Make beautiful UIs
  • Design better APIs
  • Improve code readability
  • Clean up the code

Chunking

Split information into chunks.

  • Chunk = unit of information in short-term memory
  • People recall, retain and use information much better when the info is chunked

Never write code like this!
Split your code in chunks (functions, classes, modules).

Chunking

  • Split content in chunks
  • Split functionality in chunks
  • Split code in modules, classes, functions
  • Split large functions in many small functions with fewer parameters

The usability of a system is improved when
similar parts are expressed in similar ways.

Consistency enables people to

Errors

An action or omission of action
yielding an unintended result

Most accidents are not caused by human error,
they are caused by design errors.

  • Designs should help people avoid errors and minimize the negative consequences of errors when they do occur (Forgiveness)
  • The greater the effort to accomplish a task, the less likely the task will be accomplished successfully (Performance load)

There are two basic types of errors: slips and mistakes.

Slips

  • Errors of execution
  • Unintended actions
  • Result of automatic, unconscious processes

Minimize slips by

  • Providing clear feedback on actions.
  • Use constraints and affordances
  • Using confirmations to interrupt the flow and verify the action

Mistakes

  • Errors of intention
  • Action is intended but inappropriate
  • Result of conscious mental processes

On the morning of Saturday, January 13, 2018, a ballistic missile alert was issued over television, radio, and cellphones in the state of Hawaii.

Because of a human error.
Instead of clicking the test alert link, somebody clicked the real alert link.

Because of bad design that could easily made better.

Minimize mistakes by

  • Increasing situational awareness
  • Reducing environmental noise
  • Provide corrective actions, if possible
  • Make error messages clear, and include the consequences of the error
  • Using confirmations that require multiple steps to verify the intention of highly critical tasks.

Five Hat Racks

There are five ways to organize information: category, time, location, alphabet, and continuum.

  • Category
  • Time
  • Location
  • Alphabet
  • Continuum

How are these graphs organized? They are not.
Use alphabetical order by default.

Organize data in one of the five ways to

  • Improve design understanding
  • Reduce cognitive dissonance
  • Influence the way people think about and interact with a design

Flexibility-Usability Tradeoff

As the flexibility of a system increases,
its usability decreases.

  • The time it takes to make a decision increases as the number of alternatives increases. (Hick's Law)
  • The level of control provided by a system should be related to the proficiency and experience levels of the people using the system.

Flexible designs

  • Fits more requirements
  • Performs more functions but less efficiently
  • Complex, harder to use
  • Preferred when the system usage is hard to anticipate

Inflexible designs

  • Fits few requirements
  • Performs limited functions but very efficiently
  • Simple, easier to use
  • Preferred when the system usage is clear

Flexibility-Usability Tradeoff

  • Start with the most simple design
  • Complicate only if necessary
  • Design for the simple, common use case
  • Allow parameters, configurations or controls for advanced use cases
  • Provide both GUI and CLI
  • Use progressive disclosure for advanced use cases

Hierarchy of Needs

A successful design meets people's basic needs
before it can attempt to satisfy higher level needs

Design's Hierarchy of Needs

Good designs follow the hierarchy of needs principle.

Poor designs skip various levels.

Functionality

  • Meets the most basic design requirements
  • Designs at this level are perceived to be of no value.

There are designs that fail even to function.
Do not do this!

Reliability

  • Stable and consistent performance
  • Designs at this level are perceived to be of low value.

Do not sacrifice reliability!
Never build a Three wheel car Robin

Design things to be more reliable!

Usability

  • How easy and forgiving a design is to use.
  • Designs at this level are perceived to be of moderate value.

Do not sacrifice usability!
Do not use dark patterns.

Design things to be more usable!

Proficiency

  • Do things better than they could previously.
  • Designs at this level are perceived to be of high value.

Doing things better doesn't mean doing more things!

Proficient designs can be usable too!

Creativity

  • Interacting with the design in innovative ways.
  • Designs at this level are perceived to be of highest value.
  • These designs often achieve cult-like loyalty among users.

Extreme creativity, pack a console in a mobile phone!

An iconic example of creative design.

  • Implement key functionality (and fix existing broken functionality)
  • Fix crashes, preserve data integrity and user privacy, minimise down time.
  • Address annoying lag, choppy performance, inconsistencies
  • Relentlessly optimise and refine
  • Deligh users with surprise, originality, beauty and usefulness

Mental Model

People understand and interact with designs based on
mental representations developed from experience

  • When people are exposed to something new, they imagine how it works based on their existing mental models
  • People try to map something new to something they already know
  • They interact with new design based on what they think it is

  • Use standard or common mental models if possible (aim for consistency, use same ideas, patterns)
  • Do code reviews, demos, gather feedback
  • Watch people interact with the design (public alpha, beta releases)

Modularity

Managing complexity by dividing large systems
into multiple, smaller self contained systems

Chunking principle tells us to split information at all levels.

Modularity principle tells us to make systems from self-contained smaller systems.

  • It's about identifying functional clusters of similarity in systems, and then transforming them into independent self-contained systems (modules)
  • Modules hide their internal complexity and interact with other modules through simple interface
  • A modular system reduces system complexity which improves reliability, flexibility, and maintainability
  • A modular design encourages innovation and improvement of its modules

Modularity

  • Use it when designing or modifying complex systems
  • Consider the incremental modularization of existing systems, especially during maintenance and product updates
  • Modular systems are significantly harder to design
  • Do not attempt complex modular designs without a good understanding of the system

Ockham's Razor

Given a choice between equivalent designs,
the simplest design should be selected

“Entities should not be multiplied without necessity.” - William of Ockham
“Nature operates in the shortest way possible.” - Aristotle
“Everything should be made as simple as possible, but not simpler.” - Einstein

Choose the simplest design

  • Evaluate and select among multiple, functionally equivalent designs
  • Evaluate each element within the selected design and remove as many as possible without compromising function
  • Minimize the expression of the remaining elements as much as possible without compromising function
  • Improve signal-to-noise ratio

Redundancy

Use more elements than necessary to maintain the
performance of a system in the event of failure.

  • System failure = system cannot achieve a goal.
  • Inevitable, some elements within a system will fail.
  • But, a whole system failure can be prevented using redundant elements.
  • Diverse redundancy:
    use of multiple elements of different types
    (e.g. save on local disk, cloud, paper)
  • Homogenous redundancy:
    use of multiple elements of a single type (e.g. multiple deployment zones )
  • Active redundancy:
    use of multiple elements all the time
    (e.g. multiple servers with load balancer in front)
  • Passive redundancy:
    use of replacement elements
    (e.g. replace broken server instances)
  • Use diverse redundancy for critical systems when the causes of failure cannot be anticipated
  • Use homogenous redundancy when the causes of failure can be anticipated

  • Use active redundancy for critical systems that must maintain stable performance
  • Use passive redundancy for noncritical elements within systems, or when interruptions are tolerable

Visibility

The usability of a system is improved when its status
and methods of use are clearly visible.

Three Mile Island
Incident

4:00 AM, a malfunction

Alarms are blaring, lights are flashing...

Critical system feedback is routed to a printer that can only print 15 lines a minute!

Status information is more than an hour behind.

  • Clearly indicate the system status
  • Clearly indicate the actions that can be performed, and their consequences.
  • Immediately acknowledge user actions with clear feedback
  • Use hierarchical organization and context sensitivity to minimize complexity and maximize visibility
